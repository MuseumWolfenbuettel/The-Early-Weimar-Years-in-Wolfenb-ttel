18.The Early Weimar Years in Wolfenbüttel

The revolution reached Wolfenbüttel on November 8, 1918. At the initiative of members of the Braunschweig Workers' and Soldiers' Council, a meeting was held at the "Zum Löwen" hotel to elect a Workers' and Soldiers' Council for Wolfenbüttel. This council sought to claim administrative and political power within the municipality. Subsequently, the Workers' and Soldiers' Council coexisted alongside the Wolfenbüttel City Council, which had been formed under the conditions of the unequal three-class suffrage system of the Duchy. Following the democratic municipal elections held on December 15, 1918—which were boycotted by the far-left Communist Party of Germany (KPD)—the Bourgeois Unified List emerged victorious with 54.53% of the vote (10 seats). The Social Democrats, whose support base was primarily among the working class, secured 45.47% of the vote (8 seats). The city parliament retained its dominant position, particularly as the Reich government’s military intervention through the Freikorps also targeted the suppression of the council movement. The Workers' Council of Wolfenbüttel, last elected on March 30, 1919, lost its legal foundation with the enactment of the Weimar Constitution in August 1919 and was subsequently dissolved. Representative parliamentary democracy prevailed across all levels of government, from the city council to the Reichstag. However, the opportunity for a democratic fresh start was squandered, torn apart by revolutionary illusions, revisionist militarism, monarchism, and right-wing conservatism.
